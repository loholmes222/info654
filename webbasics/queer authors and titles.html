While there are 20 known queer women that wrote lesbian pulp fictions, tracking their pseudonyms and titles have proved difficult, especially since various men that wrote pulps used feminine names to increases sales. The idea of authorship with lesbian pulp fiction is complicated as a result. In addition, the concept of queerness has evolved over the last seventy years to where various authors of lesbian pulp that would have called themselves different from heterosexual people, identities change over time. It is very likely that some authors would not call themselves queer or lesbian then or now, had they the language to describe their affect. It is unwise to ascribe an identity to someone that might not connect with those words or phrases, leading to a fascinating study of the rapidly changing queer community and its understanding of itself over the 20th and 21st centuries.

Covers of lesbian pulp fiction are always of conventionally attractive white women. The so-called "butch," or the masculine woman will have short hair and wear pants, but not always. The marketing of lesbian pulp fiction reinforces midcentury values of whiteness, femininity, gender roles, etc.
