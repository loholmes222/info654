Brief History

During post-WWII USSR, the state attempted to tightly control popular youth culture and shun Western influence. Despite Soviet attempts, Western music, mainly jazz (1940s and 1950s) and rock music (1960-1990s) seeped through the Iron Curtain to create an underground, illegal music evolution and communities, largely in major cities like Leningrad (now St. Petersburg), Moscow and Sverdlovsk (now Yekaterinburg).

The Soviet Union’s Ministry of Culture quickly realized the state's inability to stem the tide of Western music and eventually attempted to tame rock music’s influence by infusing state approved, Soviet value-drenched rock music beginning in the 1980s with state sanctioned (and censored) music, bands, and songs. The state controled recording label, called Melodiya, had total control over all official music releases until the Societ Union's fall in 1991. Benefits of being a state controlled band, called vocal instrument assemblies (VIA) was access to quality instruments and equipment shipped from the West and financial stability. The underground musicians created DIY instruments out of telephones and furniture, while maintaining full time jobs, many times as janitors or security personnel for more hour flexibility. These two rock communities circulated simultaneously during the 1980s, a decade generally regarded as the golden age of Russian rock music.

Russian rock music infused Russian folk elements with jazz and Western rock music, creating a unique sound and adapting to their own experiences and access. Borrowing from bands such as Queen, Deep Purple, and the Beatles, Russian rock music imitated but tailored their sound, many times for political rebellion against VIAs and the Ministry of Culture. When rock music was finally infused into official popular culture, as the Soviet Union began to fall, rock music became widely available.

Since the fall of the USSR in 1991, rock music has also dropped off of popular culture. Seen as a rather dead genre, some Russian rock still lingers with bands such as Shary and others touring around Russia, but rarely in Europe or other continents.
